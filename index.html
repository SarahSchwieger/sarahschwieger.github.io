Sarah Schwieger

Plant Ecologist with an interest in plant responses to climate warming.

I am an ecologist with expertise in experimental research, data synthesis, and large-scale analysis, focusing on plant-soil interactions and ecosystem processes. My research spans Arctic and temperate ecosystems, with an emphasis on understanding how environmental changes, such as climate warming and land management, influence plant and soil dynamics. I have experience leading collaborative projects and managing extensive ecological datasets, contributing to insights that inform climate change mitigation strategies.

I'm currently a Postdoctoral Fellow at Umeå University. My research focuses on the effect of climate warming on plant litter decomposition and the emission of carbon dioxide on a local (Arctic tundra) and global scale.

Research and Interests

My main interest lies in understanding how climate warming is affecting plants and how changes in plant communities with warming, such as shrubification, will affect the future of ecosystems through changes in plant production, litter decomposition, and changes in growing season length. My research spans from case studies in temperate peatlands to large-scale circum-polar synthesis of the effect of warming on carbon dioxide emissions in Arctic and alpine tundra and global collaborative research on plant decomposition under climate change.

Tundra Flux Database

As a core team member of the Tundra Flux Database, I managed and updated a database that holds over 77,000 carbon dioxide and methane flux entries from 85 Arctic and alpine tundra sites. In our paper published in >Nature, we show that a mean temperature increase of 1.4°C could lead to a 30% rise in ecosystem respiration, with soil conditions being key drivers.

Feel free to contact us with any questions about the Tundra Flux Database.

Environmental Conditions Modulate Warming Effects on Plant Litter Decomposition Globally

In this global meta-analysis of 109 warming studies, we found that a minimum of 5.2°C warming is required for a significant increase in decomposition rates globally. Future shifts in vegetation towards plants with lower litter quality, those more sensitive to warming, could enhance carbon release and slow the accumulation of organic matter in the soil. These changes may have profound implications for global carbon cycling and long-term carbon storage under ongoing climate change.

WARMUP

WARMUP investigates the hidden world of plant root decomposition under climate warming. This study focuses on root decomposition in Arctic and alpine shrubs and grasses, crucial for understanding carbon cycling and the impact of warming on global carbon emissions.
