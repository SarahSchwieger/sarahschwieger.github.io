Sarah Schwieger

Plant Ecologist with a strong interest in plant responses to climate warming in Artcic and alpine ecosystems.

I am an ecologist with expertise in experimental research, data synthesis, andlarge-scale analysis, focusing on plant-soil interactions and ecosystem processes. My research spans Arctic and temperate ecosystems, with an emphasis on understanding how environmental changes, such as climate warming and land management, influence plant and soil dynamics. I have experience leading collaborative projects and managing extensive ecological datasets, contributing to insights that inform climate change mitigation strategies.

I'm currently a Postdoctoral Fellow at Umeå University. My research focusses on the effect of climate warming on plant litter decompositon and the emission of carbon dioxide on a local (Arctic tundra) and global scale.

Research and Interests

My main interest lies in understanding how climate warming is affecting plants and how in reverse changes in plant community structure, such as shrubification will affect the future of ecosystems through changes in plant community composition, plant production, litter decomposition and phenology (e.g., changes in grwoing season length). My research interests and projects range from case stduies in temperate peatlands to understand the peat forming potential of rewetted and drained peatlands to large scale circum-polar synthesis of the effect of warming on carbon dioxide emissions in Arctic and alpine tundra and global collaborative research to better understand the global patterns of plant decomposition in response to climate change.

Tundra Flux Database

As a member of the core-team of the ‘Tundra Flux Database’, I am engaged in the management and updating of a database, which holds over 77,000 carbon dioxide and methane flux entries from 85 arctic and alpine tundra sites. In our paper on the environmental drivers of increased ecosystem respiration in warming tundra, published in Nature we show that a mean temperature increase of 1.4°C could result in a 30% rise in ecosystem respiration with soil conditions (e.g., nitrogen concentration and pH) being key drivers.

Environmental Conditions Modulate Warming Effects on Plant Litter Decomposition Globally

Global climate change poses a challenge to understanding how environmental warming affects ecosystem processes, especially plant litter decomposition. In this study, we conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis of 109 experimental warming studies from seven continents, covering diverse climates and ecosystems. Our goal was to assess the overall impact of warming on litter decomposition and to uncover key moderating factors.

The results reveal that a minimum of 5.2°C warming is required for a significant global increase in decomposition rates. Interestingly, while warming had no significant effect on decomposition at the global scale, we found clear regional patterns. Decomposition decreased in warmer, drier regions, yet showed a slight, non-significant increase in colder, wetter areas. This finding is particularly important as higher-latitude regions, which store vast amounts of terrestrial carbon, have experienced rapid warming over the past decade.

Moreover, our study suggests that future shifts in vegetation towards plants with lower litter quality—those more sensitive to warming—could enhance carbon release and slow the accumulation of organic matter in the soil. These changes may have profound implications for global carbon cycling and long-term carbon storage under ongoing climate change.

WARMUP

WARMUP will investigate the hidden world beneath the Arctic and alpine tundra, focusing on how climate warming affects plant root decomposition. This research is crucial because up to 80% of the plant biomass of these globally important biomes is hidden below the ground in the form of roots, storing vast amounts of carbon. When roots decompose, they release carbon dioxide, contributing to global warming. The Arctic and alpine regions are warming faster than the global average, and decomposition is a temperature-sensitive process. However, root decomposition under climate warming remains poorly understood, creating a critical knowledge gap that could lead to underestimating climate change impacts on carbon emissions. WARMUP will address this by comparing warming effects on root vs. leaf decomposition in Arctic and alpine shrubs and grasses, key plant types that are driving the current ‘greening’ trends emerging across the Arctic and alpine biomes.
